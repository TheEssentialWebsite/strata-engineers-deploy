Soil Conditions in St. George: Understanding the Impact of 'Blue Clay

In the St. George area, one of the most challenging soil types for construction is known as "Blue Clay." Despite its name, this soil-like material isn't always blue—it often appears purple, gray, or even tan. The name is more historical than descriptive, and what really matters is how this soil behaves when it's exposed to water from the building area as it is developed.

If you’re planning to build in Southern Utah, Blue Clay is not just a nuisance—it’s a serious geologic hazard that can do lasting damage to foundations, slabs, and hardscapes. Left unaddressed, it can crack walls, tilt patios, and disrupt structural alignment. That’s why understanding how this material behaves—and planning for it early—is one of the smartest decisions you can make during site development.

"Blue Clay" is a bedrock formation that, when exposed to the elements, looks more like soil than "bedrock." If the blue clay material hasn't been excavated and used as fill it is typically found near the slopes around the valleys, or like in some parts of Santa Clara, where the bedrock formation is close to the existing surface. In these arid areas, the clay is often dry at the time of excavation. But that dryness can be deceptive. When moisture becomes available—whether from irrigation, poor drainage, heavy rain, or plumbing leaks—this clay can expand dramatically, sometimes increasing in volume by more than 20%.

Why Expansive Soils Matter

Expansive clays like Blue Clay can cause significant structural challenges when they absorb moisture and expand. This expansion exerts pressure in all directions but is particularly disruptive when it pushes upward against foundations or sideways on retaining or basement walls. The result is often uneven lifting, known as differential movement, which places strain on the structure above.

These stresses can lead to cracked foundations, warped floors, or separated finishes. Doors and windows may no longer operate smoothly, and hardscape features like patios and driveways might shift or tilt. Even minor moisture changes—such as from seasonal rain, improper irrigation, or a slow plumbing leak—can trigger movement over time.

In many cases, the damage doesn't appear until months or even years after construction, making it easy to overlook the soil's role in long-term performance. That’s why evaluating expansive conditions like Blue Clay before breaking ground is critical to minimizing future risk.

What to Expect During Site Development

When Blue Clay is present—or suspected—there are several standard strategies to reduce risk:

  • Site Evaluation: A thorough soil evaluation by a competent geotechnical engineering firm is often the first step. While Strata Consulting Engineers does not conduct geotechnical testing in-house, we can refer clients to qualified professionals and help interpret the results.

  • Moisture Management: Proper drainage is critical. Water should be directed away from the structure through grading, gutters, and subsurface systems.

  • Structural Planning: Engineers may recommend foundation systems designed to accommodate or resist soil movement. Options could include post-tensioned slabs, slender preconstruction pile or pier extended into non-expansive material, deepened footings, or structural reinforcements.

  • Material Handling: If Blue Clay is excavated, the removed soil may be stabilized chemically, replaced with non-expansive fill, compacted and conditioned to reduce future movement, or as directed by the geotechnical report for the property.

These techniques are designed to create a more uniform, predictable foundation environment—minimizing the potential for damage down the road.

Things to Consider Before You Build

Blue Clay is a significant natural building hazard that can tear apart buildings that are not properly prepared for the condition. For anyone building in Washington County or the surrounding area, it's worth asking a few key questions:

  • Has the lot been evaluated for expansive soils?
  • Will the foundation be sitting on natural, fill, or mixed soils?
  • What drainage features are planned to keep water away from the structure?
  • Are you working with professionals who understand the local soil conditions?

The earlier these questions are addressed, the better the long-term outcome for the structure.
